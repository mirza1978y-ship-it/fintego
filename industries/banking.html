To be done right, banking IT transformation must stand on these five pillars:

● Continuity. Avoid interrupting your business processes during transformation. Your banking software solutions have to be continuously maintained and updated afterward
● Collaboration. Involve all stakeholders in devising the digital transformation strategy. It has to include a sound assessment of the project scope, costs, and timeline
● Standardization. Avoid data silos, a fractured digital ecosystem, and incompatible goals by standardizing your systems and eliminating the complexity of legacy systems
● Governance. Establish the responsibility for digital transformation efforts and introduce clear goals and objectives that align with your organization’s mission and vision
● Expertise. Partner with an expert in digital transformation and banking software services. Their domain-specific expertise will help you avoid common pitfalls and get a solution corresponding to your needs

The following four trends are to define 2024 in banking:

● Customer engagement and experience: In 2024, the acceleration of enhanced onboarding is anticipated, with a particular emphasis on digital experiences and the "consumerization of corporate banking," incorporating methods such as personalization, embedded servicing capabilities, and intelligent contact centers, as illustrated by a bank's reimagined corporate onboarding focusing on "moments that matter" for clients and servicing teams.
● Open banking: In response to the rise of open banking, embedded finance, and banking as-a-Service (BaaS), 75% of global banks have articulated a clear engagement strategy for the open ecosystem, with major banks now actively participating in digital B2B markets and platforms to provide financial services, while emphasizing the importance of robust risk frameworks and proactive engagement with regulators in the evolving landscape.
● Cards and payments: Banks navigating challenges in cards and payments, including compliance issues and cost pressures, must prioritize substantial improvements, embrace ISO 20022 for comprehensive financial messaging, deploy AI across the payment life cycle while addressing conflicts of interest, and swiftly adapt to emerging forms of money, discerning their client value and integration into propositions.
● Product and capability innovation: Leveraging small-business banking data for actionable insights, banks should evolve into trusted advisors, emphasizing customer-centric workflows beyond traditional banking, while in trade services, the evolving technology landscape demands enhanced data-oriented capabilities for improving treasury operations and working capital, transforming cash management data to foster more advisory treasury relationships.
● Emerging technology: As generative AI becomes a consumer service, banks are transitioning from exploration to proof of concept, harnessing its potential to combine artificial intelligence with human intelligence for content generation, quick problem-solving, and improved decision-making, while the private sector outpaces central banks in developing digital money, and there is a call for awareness and understanding of quantum computing programs in the corporate banking and tech sectors.
